Smith v. Johnson Corporation

No. 23-4567

UNITED STATES COURT OF APPEALS FOR THE NINTH CIRCUIT

Argued and Submitted April 10, 2024
Filed June 15, 2024

Before: RODRIGUEZ, CHEN, and WILLIAMS, Circuit Judges.

OPINION

RODRIGUEZ, Circuit Judge:

Plaintiff Jane Smith appeals the district court's grant of summary judgment in favor of defendant Johnson Corporation on her claims of employment discrimination under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, 42 U.S.C. § 2000e et seq., and retaliation under 42 U.S.C. § 2000e-3(a). We have jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1291. We reverse and remand.

I. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

Smith was employed by Johnson Corporation as a senior engineer from January 2018 to March 2023. During her tenure, she received consistently positive performance reviews, including ratings of "exceeds expectations" in fiscal years 2019 through 2022. See McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792 (1973) (establishing burden-shifting framework for Title VII claims).

In September 2022, Smith filed an internal complaint with Johnson's Human Resources department alleging that her supervisor, Mark Davis, had made repeated discriminatory comments regarding her gender. Specifically, Smith documented twelve incidents between June and September 2022 in which Davis questioned her technical competence using gender-based stereotypes.

II. STANDARD OF REVIEW

We review de novo the district court's grant of summary judgment. Vasquez v. County of Los Angeles, 349 F.3d 634, 639 (9th Cir. 2003). We must determine whether, viewing the evidence in the light most favorable to the nonmoving party, there are genuine issues of material fact and whether the district court correctly applied the relevant substantive law. Id.

III. ANALYSIS

Under the McDonnell Douglas framework, Smith must first establish a prima facie case of discrimination. She must show that: (1) she belongs to a protected class; (2) she was qualified for her position; (3) she suffered an adverse employment action; and (4) similarly situated employees outside her protected class were treated more favorably. 411 U.S. at 802.

The district court erred in concluding that Smith failed to establish the fourth element. The record contains substantial evidence that male engineers with comparable or lesser qualifications received promotions during the same period that Smith was denied advancement.

CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, we REVERSE the judgment of the district court and REMAND for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.
