Attention Mechanisms in Large Language Models: A Survey

Wei Chen1, Arun Patel2, Sarah Mueller1

1Stanford University, 2MIT

Submitted: January 20, 2024 arXiv:2401.12345 [cs.CL]

Abstract

Attention mechanisms have become the foundational building block of modern large language models (LLMs). Since the introduction of the Transformer architecture by Vaswani et al. (2017), self-attention has undergone significant evolution to address challenges in computational efficiency, context length, and representational capacity. This survey provides a comprehensive review of attention mechanism variants used in contemporary LLMs, including multi-head attention (MHA), grouped-query attention (GQA), multi-query attention (MQA), and recent proposals for linear and sub-quadratic attention. We analyze 150 papers published between 2020 and 2024, categorizing approaches by their computational complexity, memory requirements, and empirical performance across standard benchmarks. Our analysis reveals that while quadratic attention remains dominant in production systems, hybrid approaches combining local and global attention patterns show the most promise for scaling to longer contexts.

1. Introduction

The Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) introduced self-attention as a mechanism for modeling dependencies between all pairs of positions in a sequence. Unlike recurrent architectures that process sequences step by step, self-attention computes relationships between all positions simultaneously, enabling better parallelization during training.

However, the quadratic computational and memory complexity of standard self-attention—O(n²d) where n is sequence length and d is the model dimension—has motivated extensive research into more efficient alternatives.

2. Background

Standard multi-head attention computes:

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QK^T / √d_k)V

where Q, K, V are the query, key, and value matrices projected from the input sequence, and d_k is the dimension of each head.
